Go1 Autonomous Navigation:
From Imitation Learning to Social AI

A two-phase system that evolves from real-time learned control to VLM-based social decision making.

This project first deploys a real-time imitation-learning controller on Unitree Go1, then extends it with a VLM-based decision layer for human-aware navigation. The final system combines a fast controller with a slower semantic reasoning module.

Phase 1 focuses on on-robot execution with compact visual policies. Phase 2 reframes the problem as an offline social-navigation benchmark using pretrained VLMs. The VLM path is presented as advisory and safety-projected, not as raw closed-loop robot control.

Phase 1: Real-Time Imitation Learning Navigation

I collected Go1 rosbag data under teleoperation, extracted image and motion pairs, trained visual navigation policies, and deployed them as real-time ROS inference nodes. This phase emphasized low latency, smoothing, safety clipping, and stable on-robot behavior.

Transition: Why a Semantic Decision Layer Was Needed

Once the robot could execute low-level movement, the harder issue became deciding what to do around people. Social scenes require reasoning about position, motion, crossing direction, and uncertainty. That is where imitation-style low-level outputs become difficult to interpret and audit.

Phase 2: VLM Social Navigation Capstone

I built an offline benchmark that extracts front-camera image sequences from 13 curated Go1 rosbags and evaluates pretrained VLMs as high-level social decision policies. The main contribution is a prompt-policy layer that expands the action space to STOP / FORWARD / LEFT / RIGHT / REVIEW and uses sequence-based reasoning instead of pure single-frame classification.

From Geometry to Semantics

The project evolves by keeping the low-level safety signals, then adding a semantic layer for human-aware decision making.

Phase 1 Signals

What these signals do: traversability, obstacle risk, and stability help the robot move safely and react quickly.

  • Useful for reactive navigation and low-level motion safety
  • Good at answering whether the robot can move through local space

Why Semantics Was Added

Why it matters: low-level risk signals do not explain social intent around humans.

  • They do not capture crossing direction, yielding behavior, or ambiguity
  • The VLM layer adds semantic interpretation on top of those signals

Phase 2: Social Decision Benchmark

Phase 1 answered whether learned perception could run on the robot. Phase 2 asked whether pretrained VLMs could support structured social decisions for human-aware navigation in an offline benchmark.

Key Results
  • 13 curated social navigation scenarios
  • 0.60 bag-level decision accuracy on primary scenarios
  • 10-frame sequence window with 5-frame VLM input
Interpretation
  • VLM prompting enables structured social decisions beyond binary stop/go
  • Performance is still limited by perception reliability in crossings and late-entry scenes
  • VLM latency and uncertainty prevent direct low-level real-time control

In the saved final_policy_v2_crossing benchmark, both sequence methods reached 0.60 primary-case accuracy across the 10 non-review bags. The VLM phase should be read as an offline social-navigation benchmark rather than a fully deployed closed-loop controller.

What the Numbers Mean

  • Where VLM helped: receding-person cases benefited from temporal reasoning.
  • Where it struggled: crossing and entering-late cases still often collapsed to unsafe FORWARD.
  • Why REVIEW matters: it gives the policy an explicit uncertainty channel instead of forcing brittle answers.

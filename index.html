Overview

One continuous Go1 research story: first learn to move robustly in real time, then learn to interpret human scenes more semantically and safely.

Phase 1: Learning to Move

What this does: trains and deploys a real-time imitation-learning controller for low-level Go1 navigation behavior.

  • Uses Go1 rosbag + teleoperation supervision
  • Works with reactive signals such as traversability, obstacle risk, and stability
ResNet-18GRU / HistoryROS

Why Move Beyond Imitation Learning?

Why the earlier setup was not enough: imitation learning can reproduce observed motion, but it does not make crossing direction, yielding behavior, or uncertainty explicit.

  • Binary stop/go is too coarse for crossing and ambiguity
  • The robot needs a clearer semantic reason for yielding or deferring

Final System

Fast Controller + Slow Semantic Reasoner

Goal: keep the fast controller for execution, and add a slower layer for cases where human motion changes the meaning of the scene.

  • Fast controller remains responsible for real-time safety and motion
  • VLM layer provides slower advisory social decisions and review signals

What changed in Phase 1

  • Robot-side runtime stability became the main constraint.
  • Smoothing, clipping, and latency mattered as much as model quality.

What broke the old framing

  • Person-dependent scenes were not well represented by stop/go outputs.
  • Crossings, receding motion, and ambiguity kept collapsing together.

What Phase 2 changed

  • The action space expanded to STOP / FORWARD / LEFT / RIGHT / REVIEW.
  • The VLM layer stayed offline-first and advisory rather than executable.

From Geometry to Semantics

The project evolves by keeping the low-level safety signals, then adding a semantic layer for human-aware decision making.

Phase 1 Signals

What these signals do: traversability, obstacle risk, and stability are useful for keeping the robot moving without obvious collisions or unstable behavior.

  • Useful for reactive navigation and low-level motion safety
  • Good at answering whether the robot can move through local space

Why Semantics Was Added

Why they were not enough: a high obstacle score can tell you something is in front of the robot, but not whether that person is moving away, crossing leftward, or entering too late to infer intent.

  • They do not capture crossing direction, yielding behavior, or ambiguity
  • The VLM layer adds semantic interpretation on top of those signals

Phase 2: Social Decision Benchmark

Phase 1 answered whether learned perception could run on the robot. Phase 2 asked whether pretrained VLMs could support structured social decisions for human-aware navigation in an offline benchmark.

Key Results
  • 13 curated social navigation scenarios
  • 0.60 bag-level decision accuracy on primary scenarios
  • 10-frame sequence window with 5-frame VLM input
Interpretation
  • The five-action interface exposes decisions that a binary stop/go policy cannot represent
  • Crossings and late-entry cases still fail mainly because person activation is unreliable
  • Latency and missing geometric guarantees rule out direct low-level VLM control

What the Benchmark Actually Shows

  • Where it helped: receding-person cases improved when the model saw short temporal windows instead of one still frame.
  • Where it still failed: several crossing and entering-late bags still collapsed to unsafe FORWARD predictions.
  • Why REVIEW matters: it gives the evaluator a place to put genuine uncertainty instead of pretending every scene has a confident action.

Engineering Takeaways

The most useful outcome of this work was not one final accuracy number. It was learning where each layer helps, where it fails, and what should or should not be trusted in a real robot stack.

What changed architecturally

  • The early stack assumed that if the controller output was good enough, the navigation problem was mostly solved.
  • The social-navigation bags broke that assumption because the missing piece was often representation, not only control quality.
  • The final split came from that failure: fast control below, slower interpretation above, with a safety boundary in between.

What the experiments made clear

  • Reactive signals are still necessary, but they leave several person-dependent scenes underspecified.
  • Sequence context helps in some bags, especially receding motion, but the crossing failures show that better temporal wording alone is not enough.
  • Any future online use of the VLM layer would need explicit projection and probably a clearer contract with the lower-level planner.
